"Number is the ruler of forms and ideas." — Plato
Bede
Emma, you know that 7 × 8 is 56. But why? If I gave you 7 bags and each held 8 apples, how would you count them without multiplying?
Emma
I'd count 8, 16, 24, 32… like skip-counting by 8?
A Thought to Consider
Exactly the right instinct. So multiplication is just skip-counting — you found the pattern yourself. Now: if you had 7 × 9, you could start from 7 × 8. How would you get there?
Emma
Add one more 7? So 56 + 7 = 63.
You Found It
Yes. You just discovered why the times table is a table — each row is one more group. You don't need to memorise it; you can rebuild any row from the one before.

James, you went outside this morning. Did you notice anything on the leaves — on the underside especially?
James
There were little drops of water! Even though it didn't rain.
A Thought to Consider
That water didn't fall from the sky — the leaf made it. How do you suppose a leaf could make water appear from the air overnight?
James
Maybe the cold makes the air wet? Like when a cold cup gets drops outside?
You Found It
That is exactly it — you noticed the same pattern on two different things and connected them. Scientists call it condensation. You named it yourself.

Arithmetic — Slate & Chalk
The color of blackboards and chalk dust. Numbers have their own gravity — cool, precise, patient. Blue answers on a slate ground. Bede asks why, not just what.
Natural Science — Forest & Leaf
The color of living things — moss, fern, and condensation on a cold leaf. Science sessions begin outdoors, in observation. The screen is where James tells Bede what he found.
Literature — Claret & Rose
Warm claret — the color of old volumes and ink. Story has warmth, feeling, connection. The rose tint is never pink-girlish; it is the color of a well-worn book cover.
Age-Stage Adaptations
K – 2 · Ages 5–7
The Wonder Stage
Larger text (18px body), generous line spacing, voice-first input — the mic is the primary way young children talk to Bede. Narration prompts dominate: "Tell me what you noticed." Timer is per-subject (20 min), not a 2-hour block. The break screen says "Time to go outside" not "Break Time."
3 – 5 · Ages 8–10
The Question Stage
Standard text (17px), keyboard and mic both offered. Bede's Socratic questions get harder — "Why do you think so?" Handwriting canvas becomes available for math workings. Faith connections are introduced more explicitly. Emma's grade 4 session is shown here.
6 – 8 · Ages 11–14
The Argument Stage
Slightly smaller text (15px), keyboard primary. Bede introduces counter-arguments: "A skeptic would say… How would you answer?" The 60-minute block + 10-minute break cycle applies. The navy-gold-Cinzel palette feels exactly right here — serious, not childlike.
